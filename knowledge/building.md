# Building Knowledge

Comprehensive building reference for AI agents. Each section is self-contained and actionable.

---

## Site Selection

### Choosing a Build Location
Survey the area before placing a single block. Walk the footprint of your planned structure and verify it fits. Flat ground is mandatory — sloped terrain forces awkward stair-stepping in foundations. Stay at least 10 blocks from cliff edges and water bodies unless building over them intentionally.

### Clearing the Area
Remove all trees, bushes, tall grass, and surface rocks within the footprint plus a 3-block buffer. Break overhanging branches above the build height. Flatten hills by removing high blocks and filling low spots with dirt or gravel. A level build site saves hours of correction later.

### Checking for Caves Below
Dig test holes 1x1 at the four corners of your planned footprint, going 5 blocks deep. Listen for cave sounds. If you hit air, extend the hole until you find solid stone. Fill all cave openings under your foundation with cobblestone or gravel before building. Unfilled caves cause sinkhole collapses and mob spawning beneath your base.

### Spacing Between Structures
Keep at least 5 blocks between separate buildings for fire safety and navigation. Keep 8 blocks between wood structures. Use paths or fences to mark the gaps. Crowded builds look cluttered and block light.

### Biome Considerations
In deserts, avoid wood — use sandstone and terracotta. Sand foundations are unstable; replace with sandstone before building. In snowy biomes, flat roofs accumulate snow; use sloped roofs or keep the roof heated with torches. In jungles, clear a large area — jungle trees regrow aggressively and will overgrow your build.

## Foundation

### Marking the Perimeter
Place blocks (dirt or gravel, not your build material) at each corner of the planned footprint first. Stretch the outline along each wall using the corner markers as guides. Walk the outline and verify dimensions before committing to any permanent blocks. Fix the perimeter now — correcting a 1-block error after walls are built is expensive.

### Digging the Foundation Trench
Dig 1 block down along the entire perimeter outline. This creates a foundation trench. Fill the trench with stone, cobblestone, or stone bricks — materials more durable than the surrounding dirt. The recessed foundation hides the transition between dirt and wall and adds blast resistance at ground level.

### Foundation Layer Material
Use cobblestone, stone bricks, or deepslate for foundations — never wood. Wood at ground level rots aesthetically (though it doesn't decay in vanilla), looks cheap, and is flammable. Stone brick foundations give a clean visual line where the wall meets the ground.

### Flat Floor Placement
After setting the perimeter foundation, fill the interior with your chosen floor material flush with ground level. Planks, stone slabs, stone bricks, polished granite, and polished andesite all work well for floors. Slabs used as floors give a half-height raised appearance that reads as "finished floor." Dirt floors are temporary — replace them.

### Raised Platforms
For builds on uneven terrain, build a platform of stone or cobblestone first, raising everything to a uniform height. Extend the platform 1 block beyond the wall perimeter on all sides to create a visual base. Fill under the platform completely — hollow platforms echo with footstep sounds and look unfinished from outside.

## Wall Construction

### Build Corners First
Place all four corners to full wall height before filling in the wall spans. Corners anchor the proportions of the build. Use a different block temporarily at corners if needed to mark them visually. Fill spans between corners after all corners are standing.

### Standard Wall Height
A single-story wall is 4 blocks tall from the floor to the start of the roof. This gives 3 blocks of headroom after placing the floor and ceiling. A 3-block ceiling feels cramped — use 4-block walls for more comfortable interiors. Towers and feature rooms can go 6 or 8 blocks tall.

### Window Openings
Leave gaps in walls for windows as you build — do not fill them and punch out later. Standard window size is 2 blocks wide by 1 block tall, placed at eye level (2nd or 3rd block up from the floor). Taller windows (2x2) are dramatic but require a lintel block above for structural appearance. Center windows between corners for symmetry.

### Door Openings
Leave a 1-wide by 2-tall gap in the wall for standard doors. Wooden doors need clearance on the inside for swinging. Place the door opening at ground level — not raised. Double doors require a 2-wide gap with no center post.

### Wall Depth and Texture
Single-layer walls look flat. Add depth by mixing block types. Stone brick walls gain visual interest by replacing occasional blocks with cracked stone bricks or mossy stone bricks. Deepslate tile walls look modern. Alternate log orientations every few blocks. Use pilasters (protruding columns) every 4-6 blocks for long walls.

### Filling Large Wall Spans
For walls longer than 8 blocks, break the span visually with a pilaster or a change in material. A pilaster is 1 block protruding from the wall face every 6-8 blocks. Monotone long walls look like placeholder geometry.

## Roof Styles

### Flat Roof
The simplest roof. Extend the wall material or a slab 1 block beyond the wall edge on all sides, then fill the interior. Use upward-slabs or a full block layer. Add a parapet (1-block wall on the roof edge) for safety and aesthetics. Best for functional utility rooms or desert builds. Easy to walk on for adding chimneys later.

### Gabled (Sloped) Roof — Stair Method
The most common roof style. After the walls reach their full height, add 1 block on top of each long wall. Build up a ridgeline 1 block higher at center. Fill the triangular gable ends with full blocks. Cover the slopes with stairs — oak stairs, cobblestone stairs, or brick stairs all work. Lay stairs right-side-up on the lower half of each slope, upside-down on the upper half to close the gap at the peak. The peak needs a full block or slab capping the ridgeline.

### Shed Roof (Single Slope)
One wall is 1-2 blocks taller than the opposite wall. Run the roof plane as a single slope from high wall to low wall using stairs. Faster to build than a gable, and distinctive. Good for lean-to additions and workshop annexes.

### A-Frame Roof
Walls go only 2-3 blocks tall. The roof starts immediately and slopes steeply to the ground. Use stairs on the outer edges and fill interior slope faces with planks or logs. A-frames work well for small cabins and cozy aesthetics. The steep pitch means no flat interior ceiling — the roof IS the ceiling.

### Dome Approximation
Domes in Minecraft require stair-stepping since blocks are cubic. Use a center pillar as reference. Work outward in rings, stepping down 1 block every 2-3 blocks of radius, using stairs and slabs to approximate the curve. Wool or concrete give clean dome surfaces. True spheres require voxel-sphere charts or a schematic — approximate domes are faster for survival builds.

### Layered Slab Roof
For a low-profile, modern look: place upward-slabs as the entire roof. Extend 1 block beyond the walls. The thin slab edge looks like a flat concrete roof with an overhang. Combine with stone or deepslate walls for a contemporary build style.

## Doors and Entrances

### Wooden Doors
Craft from 6 planks of any type. Right-click to open and close. Players and villagers can open wooden doors. Zombies will break wooden doors on Hard difficulty. Place the door with the hinge on the preferred side by positioning yourself to control orientation. Add a step (1 block raised) at the entrance for visual framing.

### Iron Doors
Require a redstone signal to open. Cannot be opened by hand or by zombies. Always pair with a button, pressure plate, or lever for access. Stone pressure plates trigger from players and mobs — use weighted pressure plates or buttons if you want player-only access. Iron doors are mandatory for mob-proof secure areas.

### Trapdoors as Design Elements
Oak, iron, and other trapdoor variants work as shutters, vents, fence-gate alternatives, and decorative panels. Trapdoors placed on the side of a block face outward like a panel. Use them as cellar hatches, window shutters, and skylight covers.

### Entrance Framing
Frame doors with pilasters or decorative logs. Place a slab or stair above the door as a lintel. Add a small awning of slabs protruding 1-2 blocks above the entrance. Path blocks or stone slabs leading up to the door create an entrance path. Flower pots flanking the door add color without consuming space.

### Double Doors
Two doors placed side-by-side in a 2-wide gap. Both must be the same type. When you open one, the other opens automatically if they are placed facing each other. Essential for large buildings where a single door feels too narrow.

## Windows

### Glass Panes vs Glass Blocks
Glass panes are 2D thin panels that connect to adjacent blocks and panes. They look like real window glass. Glass blocks are full cubes — use them for walls where you want a thick glass look or for decorative pillars. Panes are almost always the correct choice for windows in walls.

### Placing Glass Panes
Panes connect automatically to adjacent solid blocks. In a 2-wide window opening, place one pane in each block — they connect to form a continuous window. In a 1-wide opening, the pane stands as a vertical strip. Panes placed in corners form an L-shape, which can look awkward — add a 1-block framing block at the corner instead.

### Colored Glass
Stained glass and stained glass panes come in 16 colors. Use colored glass for stained glass windows in churches, color-coded room indicators, or aesthetic accents. Yellow glass in lantern rooms gives warm light appearance. Blue glass in underwater builds reads as water. Mix colors in 1-2-block patterns for decorative windows.

### Iron Bars and Fence Alternatives
Iron bars connect like panes but are 3D and read as bars/grates. Use iron bars for prison cells, dungeon aesthetics, forge ventilation, and secure windows that admit arrows. Oak fence in a window opening reads as a rustic shutter or vent. Nether brick fence in a window reads as a fortress arrow slit.

## Lighting

### Preventing Mob Spawns with Torches
Mobs spawn in light level 0. Torches provide light level 14 at the source, falling by 1 per block. Place torches every 6-7 blocks along walls and ceilings to prevent all mob spawning. On a flat exterior surface, torches every 7 blocks in a grid prevent spawns between them. Count the blocks and be systematic — missed dark spots breed creepers.

### Indoor Torch Placement
Place wall torches at 2-3 blocks height on walls. Floor torches are tripped over. Ceiling torches work but require a block to attach to. Standard indoor scheme: one torch in each corner plus one on each long wall. For large rooms, add a central ceiling torch on a hanging block.

### Lanterns
Lanterns hang from the underside of blocks or sit on top of blocks. Light level 15 — brighter than torches. Chain lanterns hang from ceiling blocks for an elegant chandelier look. Soul lanterns produce teal light at level 10 — atmospheric but dimmer. Lanterns on walls require placing a block and attaching the lantern to its underside.

### Glowstone and Sea Lanterns
Both are full blocks with light level 15 — the maximum. Embed in floors, ceilings, or walls for flush lighting with no visible torch. Glowstone requires Nether access or witch drops. Sea lanterns require ocean monument access or prismarine farming. Both are decorative replacements for torches in high-end builds. Sea lanterns fit underwater builds. Glowstone fits Nether-aesthetic builds.

### Shroomlights and Froglight
Shroomlights (from Nether fungi) provide light level 15 and have a warm organic appearance. Frooglights (from feeding frogs glowsquid) come in pearlescent, verdant, and ochre. Both are light level 15. Use as decorative ceiling or floor lighting.

### Candles
Candles sit on top of blocks. One candle = light level 3; stack up to 4 candles for light level 12. Candles on cake = light level 3. Candles are atmospheric but weak — supplement with other light sources for mob-proofing. Candles cannot be placed on most surfaces — they need a solid flat top.

### Exterior Lighting
Line paths with lanterns on fence posts every 6-7 blocks. Place torches or lanterns above doorways. Light the roof and all exterior alcoves. Dark corners on the outside of your base spawn creepers that will blow up your walls.

## Interior Design

### Minimum Furniture Set
Every habitable space needs: one crafting table, one furnace, one chest, one bed. This is the survival minimum. Place the crafting table in a corner. Place the furnace next to it (you'll use both together). Place the chest on the opposite wall from the door. Place the bed away from the door with its head against a wall.

### Crafting and Smelting Area
Cluster the crafting table, furnace, and a chest together as a "work zone." Add a second furnace beside the first for faster smelting. Add a blast furnace for ores and a smoker for food. Add a barrel or second chest next to the furnaces for fuel and raw ore storage. Label chests with item frames showing the contents.

### Chest Placement
Chests need 1 clear block above them to open. Do not place a solid block directly above a chest — it won't open. Double chests are two single chests placed side-by-side. Chests can be placed on top of each other if they are not adjacent (diagonally offset). Use item frames on the wall above chests to label categories.

### Beds
Beds are 2 blocks long. The foot of the bed is placed first, then extends to the head block. You must sleep in a bed within 3 blocks to set spawn. Beds explode in the Nether and End — never use them there. Beds can be dyed by crafting with wool of matching color.

### Bookshelves
Bookshelves placed within 2 blocks of an enchanting table (with 1 air block between) boost enchanting power. You need 15 bookshelves surrounding the table for maximum level 30 enchants. Bookshelves also look excellent as interior wall decor. Craft from 6 planks and 3 books.

### Item Frames and Display
Item frames attach to any solid block face. Place an item in them by right-clicking with the item. Glow item frames emit light and the item inside glows. Use item frames as signs, labels, map displays, and wall art. Rotate the item inside with right-clicks.

### Flower Pots and Decoration
Flower pots are 1x1 decor blocks that hold saplings, flowers, mushrooms, ferns, bamboo, and cacti. Use them on windowsills, tables (pressure plates as tables), and shelves (slabs with item frames). A flower pot on each window sill takes 2 seconds to place and dramatically improves interior appearance.

### Banners
Banners hang from the underside of blocks or stand on the ground. Craft from 6 wool and 1 stick. Add patterns at a loom using dye and banner patterns. Use banners as faction flags, room identifiers, wall art, and directional markers. Banners placed on walls hang flat; placed on the ground stand upright.

### Carpet and Rugs
Carpet costs 2 wool per block, comes in 16 colors, and is 1/16th of a block tall. Lay carpet on floor surfaces for color, warmth, and room delineation. Use contrasting carpet colors for rugs (a 3x5 area of one color within a different-colored floor). Mobs can spawn on carpet — carpet alone does not prevent mob spawning.

## Storage Rooms

### Layout Principles
Design storage rooms in rows. Each row of double chests holds items from one category. Leave 2 blocks of aisle between opposing chest rows — 1 block is too tight to see chest labels. Label every chest with an item frame showing a representative item.

### Category Organization
Organize chests by these categories at minimum: raw materials (wood, stone, dirt), processed blocks (planks, cobblestone, bricks), ores and metals, food and farming, tools and weapons, armor, redstone components, mob drops, valuables (diamonds, gold, ancient debris). Add more categories as your inventory grows.

### Shulker Box System
Shulker boxes hold 27 stacks and retain their contents when broken. Use shulker boxes inside chests for nested storage — a chest of shulker boxes holds 27×27 stacks. Color-code shulker boxes by category. Label with item frames. Shulker boxes are the most space-efficient storage in the game.

### Barrel Alternative
Barrels open from any side, including when another block is placed on top. Use barrels in tight spaces where chests cannot be opened. Barrels hold the same as a single chest (27 slots).

### Ender Chest Storage
Ender chests have 27 slots shared across all ender chests in the world for that player. Use your ender chest for items you carry across dimensions: tools, valuables, essential blocks. Ender chest contents never drop on death. Craft from 8 obsidian and 1 eye of ender.

## Building Materials Reference

### Cobblestone
Obtained by mining stone. Light level: 0. Blast resistance: 6. Not flammable. The default utility block. Use for foundations, walls, paths, and functional structures. Upgrade to stone (smelt cobblestone) or stone bricks (craft 4 stone) for a polished look.

### Wood (Planks and Logs)
Six types in 1.21.1: oak, spruce, birch, jungle, acacia, dark oak, plus mangrove, cherry, and bamboo. Planks and logs are flammable — do not place near lava, fire, or campfires. Oak is lightest; spruce is dark and rustic; birch is pale and modern; acacia is orange and striking; dark oak is dark and solid; cherry is pink and decorative.

### Stone Bricks
Craft from 4 stone in a 2x2 grid. Variants: cracked (smelt stone bricks), mossy (combine with vines or moss blocks), chiseled. Stone bricks are the premium overworld decorative block. Blast resistance: 6. Not flammable. Use for castles, fortresses, and formal structures.

### Deepslate
Found below Y=0 and in deepslate veins. Variants: cobbled deepslate, deepslate bricks, deepslate tiles, polished deepslate, chiseled deepslate. Harder to mine than stone (tool takes 1.5x longer). Deepslate bricks and tiles have a modern, dark, premium look. Use for modern builds, underground bases, and fortress walls.

### Terracotta
Mined in badlands biomes or crafted by smelting clay blocks. Stained with dye to produce 16 colors — all muted and earthy. Hardened terracotta resists blasts well. Glazed terracotta has directional patterns that tile in interesting ways. Use for colorful facades, desert builds, and patterned floors.

### Sandstone
Crafted from 4 sand. Variants: sandstone, cut sandstone, smooth sandstone, chiseled sandstone. Smooth sandstone (smelted) has a clean pale surface. Desert temples and desert village structures use sandstone — match if building in desert biome.

### Nether Bricks
Found in Nether fortresses or crafted from nether brick items. Dark, slightly reddish. Blast resistance: 6. Variants: nether bricks, cracked nether bricks, chiseled nether bricks, nether brick fence, nether brick stairs/slabs. Use for Nether-themed builds, demonic aesthetics, and underground fortresses.

### Prismarine
Obtained from ocean monuments. Variants: prismarine, prismarine bricks, dark prismarine. Prismarine bricks have an aqua-and-green color and rotate visually over time. Sea lanterns (also from monuments) complement prismarine. Use for underwater builds, aquariums, and aquatic aesthetics.

### Blackstone
Found in the Nether (basalt deltas, bastions). Variants: blackstone, polished blackstone, polished blackstone bricks, gilded blackstone. Very dark — nearly black. Works well with gold accents (gilded blackstone). Use for dark castles, void-themed structures, and high-contrast builds.

## Slab and Stair Tricks

### Half-Slab Placement
Right-click the top half of a block to place a top slab; right-click the bottom half to place a bottom (upward) slab. A block space with both a top and bottom slab is filled as a full block but counts as two slab blocks — useful for splitting inventory. Use slabs as paving (bottom slab flush with the ground), ceilings (top slab), and details.

### Upside-Down Stairs
Aim at the upper half of a block face when placing stairs to place them upside-down. Upside-down stairs are used for archways (upside-down stair on top, right-side-up stair below), curved roof peaks, and ornate details. Rows of upside-down stairs along a ceiling give a vaulted ceiling effect.

### Stair Archways
To build an arch over a 3-wide opening: place 1 block on each side of the opening at the top. Place upside-down stairs on the inner face of each top block, pointing toward center. Place 1 upside-down stair at the apex if the opening is 3 wide. This creates a 3-wide pointed arch with no full-block ceiling.

### Waterlogged Slabs
Right-click a slab that is partially or fully submerged, or fill a slab's space with water, and it becomes waterlogged — it holds water while displaying the slab model. Use waterlogged slabs for seamless pool floors and underwater builds where you want a flat floor without visible water surface.

### Slab Overhang Roofs
Extend slabs 1-2 blocks beyond the wall perimeter using the top-slab placement. This creates an overhang with a very clean edge. Combine with a full-block parapet behind the overhang for a modernist flat-roof look.

## Fence and Wall Mechanics

### Fence Height
Fences are 1.5 blocks tall — they appear as 1 block but have a hitbox of 1.5 blocks. Most mobs cannot jump over fences (except wolves/dogs, spiders, and horses under some conditions). Players cannot jump over fences without a block to step on. This makes fences mob-proof barriers without needing to be 2 blocks tall.

### Fence Connectivity
Fences connect automatically to adjacent fences, solid blocks, and fence gates. A standalone fence post (surrounded by air) appears as a thick post with no rails. Position fences against walls or other fences to get connected rail segments. Fence corners are automatic.

### Fence Gates
Craft from 2 planks and 4 sticks. Open by right-clicking. Fence gates can be opened by villagers, and zombies can break them on Hard difficulty (same as wooden doors). Place fence gates flush with the fence line — they connect seamlessly. Iron fence gates (not in vanilla, use iron doors instead) require redstone.

### Cobblestone and Stone Walls
Wall blocks (cobblestone wall, stone brick wall, deepslate brick wall, etc.) behave like fences but have a broader connection shape. Walls connect to blocks on all 4 sides and above. Post shape vs. wall shape depends on neighbors. Walls have the same 1.5-block mob-proof height. More varied appearance than wood fences.

## Scaffolding

### Placing Scaffolding
Scaffolding is crafted from 6 bamboo and 1 string per 6 scaffolding. Place the first piece against any solid surface. Subsequent pieces can be placed extending horizontally outward up to 6 blocks without support. Scaffolding can also be stacked vertically by looking up and placing.

### Climbing Scaffolding
Walk up scaffolding by moving forward into it while holding the jump key — you ride up the side. Crouch and release to slide down. Scaffolding is fast for vertical access during construction. Remove the bottom piece of a scaffolding column to collapse the entire column in one break.

### Scaffolding Limits
Scaffolding extends up to 6 blocks horizontally from a supported edge. Beyond 6, the scaffolding turns orange/yellow indicating it will fall. When you break the outermost supported block, hanging scaffolding falls as items. Plan scaffolding in sections for very wide builds.

## Ladder Placement

### Basic Ladder Use
Ladders attach to any solid vertical block face. Craft from 7 sticks. Right-click the face of the block where you want the ladder. Players climb by pressing toward the ladder and holding jump/crouch to stop. Ladders must be continuous — a gap in the ladder chain drops you.

### Ladder Shafts
For vertical access inside walls or towers, dig or leave a 1x1 shaft and line one face with ladders from bottom to top. The shaft is compact but requires the player to enter the shaft and face the ladder wall. For wide shafts, add a second ladder on the opposite wall for faster up-and-down.

### Ladder Placement Tricks
Ladders placed on a block face that has another block directly in front do not render the ladder (the space is too tight to use). Always leave at least 1 block of clearance in front of a ladder wall. You can place a ladder on a sign attached to a block if the sign is on the correct face.

## Water Features

### Infinite Water Source
Dig a 2-block wide by 2-block long pool, 1 block deep. Fill two diagonal corners with water buckets. Both corners generate flowing water that creates new source blocks in the other corners. The pool becomes an infinite source — scoop from one corner and it refills. This is the foundation of all water features.

### Waterfall Entrances
Place a water source at height (1-2 blocks above the wall line). Let water flow down a flat wall face. You can walk through the waterfall to enter. Combine with a recessed doorway behind the waterfall for a dramatic entrance. Mobs generally do not walk through falling water.

### Moats
Dig a trench 2-3 blocks deep and 2-3 blocks wide around your base perimeter. Fill with water from an infinite source. Mobs cannot cross without a bridge. Moats also prevent creeper approaches. Combine with a drawbridge (piston-retractable bridge) for a fully functional castle moat.

### Decorative Ponds
Dig a 5x5 hole 2-3 blocks deep with irregular edges for a natural look. Fill with an infinite water source. Add sand or gravel to the bottom. Add lily pads on the surface, clay or dirt around the edges, and seagrass if underwater. A pond immediately improves any garden or courtyard.

### Water Elevators
Place soul sand at the bottom of a 1x1 water column. Soul sand under water generates upward bubble columns — step into the column to be lifted to the top. For a downward elevator, use magma blocks at the bottom instead — magma generates downward bubble columns. Cap the top of the column with a block so the water doesn't spill. This is the fastest vertical transport in the game.

## Farm Integration

### Crop Farm Basics
Crops (wheat, carrots, potatoes, beetroot) grow on farmland — dirt tilled with a hoe. Farmland must be within 4 blocks of a water source to stay hydrated. Dry farmland reverts to dirt after a time. Hydrated farmland appears darker. Layout: rows of crops 4 blocks wide on each side of a central water channel.

### Standard Farm Layout
Dig a 1-block wide channel through the center of your farm plot. Fill the channel with water (from an infinite source). Till the dirt on both sides, up to 4 blocks from the water. Plant crops. This gives an 8-wide by any-length farmable strip with one water channel. Every block of farmland is within 4 blocks of water.

### Fencing Animal Pens
Fence animal pens with 2-block-tall fences OR 1-fence-height plus 1 block wall so animals cannot jump out. Horses require 2-block-tall fences. Pigs and cows cannot jump fences. Chickens fly out of 1-block fences — always use 2-block-tall fences or covered pens for chickens. Add a fence gate for entry. Place a water trough (1 block of water) inside.

### Farm Lighting
All crops grow faster in light level 9 or higher. Place torches or lanterns every 4-5 blocks on the farm to provide light at night. Without light, crops only grow during the day and very slowly. Glowstone or sea lanterns embedded in the ground between crop rows provide flush lighting that doesn't interfere with harvesting.

### Automated Harvesting Foundation
A basic auto-farm requires pistons, observers, and a water flush channel. The observer detects crop growth, triggers pistons, which break the crops, and a water flush carries drops to a collection point. This is a redstone farm — build it only after you have redstone resources. Manual farms suffice for early game.

## Multi-Story Buildings

### Floor Spacing
Use 4 blocks from one floor to the next floor above: 1 floor block + 3 air blocks of headroom. Three blocks of headroom avoids the "very low ceiling" claustrophobia. Five blocks is spacious and works for large halls, shops, and throne rooms. Do not go below 3 blocks headroom for any inhabited space.

### Staircase Design
Internal staircases occupy a 3x3 footprint minimum for a straight run. A quarter-turn staircase uses a 3x3 footprint with a 1-block landing at the turn. The full-spiral staircase fits in a 3x3 footprint but is tight. Stairs blocks placed in a spiral (each 90 degrees rotated from the last) give the spiral effect. Add handrails (fence or fence gate blocks) along the open edge.

### Open Stair Wells
For dramatic multi-story builds, leave an open void (stair well) in the center and run stairs around the perimeter of the void. This is a standard European-style stairwell. Add glass pane railings around the void for safety and visual drama.

### Floor Differentiation
Give each story a different feel using flooring materials or ceiling height. Ground floor: stone or wooden planks. Second floor: planks with carpet. Top floor: lighter materials (birch, quartz, smooth sandstone). Different flooring instantly communicates which floor you're on.

### Load-Bearing Visual Elements
In multi-story builds, add visible columns or pilasters every 4-6 blocks along the walls. This gives the structure a sense of structural logic. Use log pillars, stone brick columns, or iron bar columns. Columns in the interior mark load points and divide large rooms visually.

## Defensive Features

### Perimeter Walls
Outer walls should be at least 4 blocks tall to prevent most mobs from jumping over. 5 blocks is safer (spider-proof if the top is sloped outward). Stone or cobblestone walls are standard. Add merlons (2-block raised sections with 1-block gaps between) along the top of a wall for a battlement appearance and cover for archers.

### Arrow Slits
Create arrow slits in walls by placing a fence post in a 1x2 opening: solid block above, fence block, solid block below. The fence block allows arrows and vision but blocks mob passage. Alternatively, use a 1-wide gap 2 blocks tall and stand behind it to shoot through. Arrow slits every 4-6 blocks along walls enable defensive fire.

### Watchtowers
Build watchtowers at wall corners — at least 2 blocks taller than the wall. 4x4 footprint minimum. Include ladders or internal stairs for access. Add battlements at the top. Watchtowers extend visibility range and allow shooting over wall-top defenders. Make the tower floor 1 block higher than the wall walk so you can see over the battlements.

### Moat Effectiveness
A 2-block-deep water moat around the base perimeter stops most ground mobs. Mobs will walk into the moat and be carried away by current. Combine a moat with a drawbridge (retractable pistons + levers) for access control. Lit moats prevent mob spawning in the water channels.

### Landmine Alternatives
Place pressure plates wired to TNT under a gravel or sand surface path outside your walls. When a mob or player steps on the path, the gravel/sand falls, the pressure plate triggers, and TNT detonates. Note: this also harms you. Use carefully and mark your own mine field mentally.

## Village-Style Buildings

### Biome-Matched Construction
Each village biome uses distinct materials. Plains villages: oak planks and logs, cobblestone. Desert villages: sandstone, cut sandstone, smooth sandstone. Savanna villages: acacia planks and logs. Taiga villages: spruce planks and logs. Snowy villages: spruce with cobblestone. Match the dominant material to blend your builds with the surrounding village aesthetic.

### Workstation Placement
Each villager profession requires a specific workstation. Blast furnace = armorer. Smoker = butcher. Barrel = fisherman. Cartography table = cartographer. Brewing stand = cleric. Composter = farmer. Fletching table = fletcher. Grindstone = weaponsmith. Lectern = librarian. Loom = leatherworker. Smithing table = toolsmith. Stonecutter = mason. Place workstations inside buildings that match the profession's trade focus.

### Bed Placement for Villagers
Villager beds must be accessible from a 1-block clearance on the side they face into (the pillow end). Do not box in a bed on all sides — villagers cannot pathfind to a sealed bed. Beds must be inside a structure (roofed) to count as a valid villager bed. Each villager needs its own unique bed.

### Village Pathways
Connect buildings with gravel paths or path blocks (shovel on grass). Paths in vanilla villages are typically 2-3 blocks wide. Gravel paths: slightly bumpy and natural. Path blocks (shovel right-click on grass): flat, textured dirt appearance that doesn't grow grass. Add torches or lanterns every 7-8 blocks along village paths.

## Path and Road Design

### Path Block Creation
Right-click dirt or grass with any shovel to create a path block. Path blocks are 15/16ths of a block tall — slightly sunken below normal terrain. They don't grow grass or dirt over time. They cannot be placed on dirt directly from inventory — must be created in place. Path blocks revert to dirt if a solid block is placed on top.

### Gravel and Cobblestone Paths
Gravel paths: place gravel 1 block wide or 2-3 blocks wide. Natural appearance. Gravel is affected by gravity — place it carefully on slopes. Cobblestone paths: more formal, grey. Best for castle approaches, market streets, and formal entrance roads. Stone slab paths (bottom slabs flush with ground): the most formal and smoothest appearance.

### Road Width Standards
Single lane path: 1 block. Standard road: 3 blocks (1 center, 1 on each side). Cart/wide road: 5 blocks. Add 1-block shoulders of grass, stone, or path on each side of a formal road. For curved roads, offset each row by 1 block every 2-3 blocks of length to approximate a curve in block form.

### Decorative Road Elements
Add street lamps (fence post + lantern on top) every 7-8 blocks. Plant flowers or shrubs (leaf blocks) at intersections. Add benches (stair block facing outward with fence post armrests). Add wells at road intersections (cobblestone wall in a ring with a bucket on an item frame).

## Bridge Construction

### Flat Bridges
The simplest bridge: solid blocks spanning from bank to bank, 3 blocks wide. Add fence or wall railings on both sides (1 block tall). Use stone, planks, or cobblestone as the deck. For spans wider than 8 blocks, add 1-wide stone support columns directly below the deck every 4-6 blocks down into the water.

### Arch Bridges
Arch bridges require building the arch from the sides and meeting in the middle. Use stairs and slabs to approximate the arch curve. The deck sits on top of the arch structure. Support columns descend from the arch sides to the riverbed. Arch bridges are visually dramatic and historically accurate. Best for large stone bridges.

### Suspension Bridge Approximation
Use fence posts as towers, then string fence blocks horizontally between towers at height, then slope down to deck level using stair or slab sections. This reads as a suspension bridge without actual redstone. Connect fence from deck railings up to the "cable" fence line. Best for wide rivers.

### Bridge Width and Railings
Bridges narrower than 3 blocks feel dangerous and cramped. Add fence or wall railings on both sides, 1 block tall — this is the minimum safe railing. Fence gates in the railing at each end of the bridge provide a visual entry point. Lanterns on fence post extensions above the railing light the bridge at night.

## Garden and Landscaping

### Flowers and Plants
Place flowers in 1x1 gaps in stone or path-block surfaces for a cobblestone-with-flowers look. Tall flowers (sunflowers, lilacs, roses, peonies) occupy 2-block-tall spaces. Place tall flowers against walls for a climbing-plant appearance. Use bonemeal on grass to generate random flowers rapidly. Mix flower types for a natural garden.

### Hedge Rows
Place leaf blocks in lines 1-2 blocks tall for hedges. Use oak, spruce, or azalea leaves. Azalea leaves drop flowers for a blooming hedge look. Leaf blocks require shears to collect — place them manually from silk-touch or shear them from trees. Trim hedges by breaking individual blocks for custom shapes.

### Pond and Water Garden
Dig an irregular-edged pond (avoid perfect rectangles). Vary depth: 1 block at edges, 2 blocks in center. Line edges with dirt, grass, sand, or clay. Add lily pads on the surface. Place seagrass inside for underwater plants. Surround with flowers and place lanterns on lily pads for light.

### Tree Placement
Space trees at least 3-4 blocks apart or they won't grow (lack of light/space). Saplings need light level 8 or above and 1-2 blocks clearance around them depending on species. Oak: compact, 1 block clearance. Birch: tall, thin. Spruce: very tall, plant in pairs for a forest look. Dark oak: wide, needs 2x2 saplings. Jungle: massive, plant away from builds.

### Terrace Gardens
On slopes, build retaining walls 2-3 blocks tall from stone or stone bricks, then fill behind the wall with dirt to create a flat terrace. Each terrace tier is 1-2 blocks lower than the previous. Plant crops, flowers, or decorative shrubs on each terrace. Terrace gardens convert unusable sloped land into productive or beautiful space.

## Common Mistakes

### Floating Builds
Every structure must connect to the ground. Floating disconnected platforms look incomplete and break immersion. If you want an elevated build, add stilts (log columns, stone columns, or arches) descending to the ground. Bridges must connect to terrain on both banks.

### Too Dark Inside
Mobs spawn in complete darkness (light level 0). A house with no interior torches will spawn zombies inside at night. Place torches during construction, not after. Aim for light level 7 or higher everywhere inside. Dark corners in basements, behind chests, and under staircases will breed mobs.

### No Mob-Proofing Outside
Lighting the interior is not enough. Light all exterior surfaces within 7 blocks of your walls. Mobs spawn on any surface with light level 0 — even on your roof, in your garden, and on paths outside your walls. Walk the perimeter at night and observe where mobs spawn. Place torches there.

### Flammable Materials Near Fire and Lava
Wood, planks, wool, leaves, and bamboo catch fire if adjacent to lava or flames. Never place wood within 4 blocks of exposed lava (fire can jump). Furnaces adjacent to wood walls are unsafe — replace the blocks on 3 sides of the furnace with stone or cobblestone. Check for lava vents below your foundation before building wood structures.

### No Chest Space
Starting a base without sufficient chest storage leads to dropped items and lost resources. Build at minimum 4 double chests in your storage area before you begin active play from the base. You will always need more storage than you think. Storage room first, decoration second.

### Monotone Walls
A wall of a single block type for 10+ blocks reads as unfinished or placeholder. Break up walls with: pilasters, windows, mixed materials, recessed sections, or block texture variants (mossy, cracked, chiseled). Vary depth — even 1 block of protrusion on a column breaks up a flat wall dramatically.

### Ignoring Ceiling Height
Ceiling height of 2 blocks (normal "safe" minimum) feels claustrophobic. Always build 3 blocks of clearance minimum. Two-block crawl spaces exist only in extreme resource scarcity. Multi-story buildings with 2-block floors feel like compression chambers. Spend the extra blocks.

### Doors Without Lighting
A doorway without exterior lighting is a mob funnel. Mobs walk to doors and wait or attack. Place a lantern, torch, or glowstone above every doorway — inside and outside. A lit doorway also reads as welcoming and active.

## Agent Build Commands

### Using !design for Structured Building
Use the !design command with a detailed description to generate and execute a building plan. Provide: dimensions (e.g., "10x8 two-story house"), material preferences ("oak wood with stone brick foundation"), style ("medieval"), and special features ("basement storage room, roof terrace"). The more detail in the description, the better the output. Example: `!design "Build a 12x10 oak wood house with stone foundations, gabled roof, basement, and a front porch"`. Review the plan before approving execution.

### Using !scan to Survey Before Building
Run !scan before placing any permanent blocks on a new site. !scan reads the terrain blocks, elevation changes, and nearby structures within the scan radius. Use the scan output to: identify where to level the ground, find cave openings that need filling, locate existing structures to match or avoid, and understand what materials are locally available. Scanning prevents unpleasant surprises like building over a mineshaft or on unstable gravel.

### Using !material to Substitute Blocks
When planned materials are unavailable, use !material to substitute similar blocks while preserving the design intent. Specify the original material and the desired replacement: `!material "replace oak planks with spruce planks in current build"`. The command applies substitutions across the in-progress structure. Use this to adapt designs to available resources without restarting.

## Material Estimation

### Block Count Formula
Estimate wall block requirements before starting: Wall blocks = 2 × (Length + Width) × Height. For a 10x8 house, 4 blocks tall: 2 × (10 + 8) × 4 = 144 wall blocks (minus door and window openings, approximately -20 to -30 blocks). Add 20% buffer for mistakes and variations. Roof blocks = 0.5 × Length × Width for a flat roof, more for pitched. Floor blocks = Length × Width. Total material: wall + roof + floor + foundation layer.

### Wood Harvesting Efficiency
One oak tree yields 4-7 logs. A 10x8 house uses approximately 100-150 oak planks (25-38 logs, 6-10 trees). Plant saplings immediately after felling any tree — replant ratio 1:1 to maintain your wood supply. Bamboo smelts 0.25 items per bamboo block — only use as bulk fuel, not wood substitute. Dark oak requires 2x2 sapling arrangement and yields 20-40 logs per tree — most efficient wood tree for large projects.

### Mixing Too Many Materials
Using 5+ different block types on one build surface creates visual chaos. Limit primary structural materials to 2-3 types. Use accent materials (stairs, slabs, fences, glass) freely since they are visually subordinate. A single primary material with 1 accent (oak planks + stone brick trim) is more effective than 4 competing materials.
