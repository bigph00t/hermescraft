# Minecraft Creative Flows — Inspiration & Objectives Library

This file is a creativity library for AI agents. Each chunk plants a seed: a goal to pursue, a style to explore, a build to attempt. The agent's LLM brain should expand on these seeds using available tools and current context.

---

## Building Projects

### Residential

### Cozy Cottage
Build a warm, lived-in cottage. Find a gentle hillside or meadow. Lay a foundation of stone bricks and cobblestone with a 6x8 or 8x10 footprint. Frame walls with oak logs and fill panels with oak planks. Add a pitched roof using stairs and slabs — asymmetric rooflines look more natural than symmetric ones. Place a campfire inside the chimney using a 1x1 stone column topped with a slab so the smoke rises through the roof. Plant flower pots on window ledges using poppy, dandelion, or oxeye daisy. Surround with a small flower garden: bone-meal a patch of dirt, let wildflowers spawn, then place a low fence to frame the yard. Add a lantern above the door for evening lighting. This build signals home.

### Medieval Castle
Claim a hilltop or cliff edge and build upward. Start with a thick outer wall: stone brick base 3 blocks wide and 8 blocks tall. Add crenellations (alternating high/low blocks) along the parapet. Place a gatehouse with a 2-wide portcullis opening — iron bars make convincing portcullis texture. Build corner towers 3x3 that rise 3 blocks above the main wall. Inside the wall: keep tower in the center, 12x12 footprint, 3 stories tall. Fill the bailey with small structures — stable (hay bales, fences), smithy (blast furnace, anvil), barracks. Dig a moat 2 blocks deep around the outer wall and fill with water. A drawbridge can be simulated with pistons or just a wood-slab ramp. This build rewards patience and stone gathering.

### Treehouse
Find a dark oak forest or jungle where trees grow tall and close together. Pick 2-4 anchor trunks within 8 blocks of each other. Build a platform between them at height 10-15 using oak planks and slabs, leaving gaps around trunk entries. Connect platforms with rope bridges: 2 fence posts on each side, oak planks for walkway, fence gates at each end. Add a 4x4 room on the main platform with a half-log ceiling for low, cozy feel. Cut hidden rooms into the treetop canopy — access through a trapdoor disguised in a leaf cluster. Hang lanterns from the underside of platforms on chains. Use leaf blocks as camouflage walls on the outer edge. Feels secret. Feels alive.

### Underground Base
Find a surface entrance — a natural ravine edge, a hillside, or just dig straight down. Design around a central corridor 3x3 wide with rooms branching left and right. Room 1 (Entry): drop shaft with ladders, chests for loot. Room 2 (Workshop): crafting table, furnaces in a row, anvil. Room 3 (Storage vault): rows of labeled chests with item frames showing contents. Room 4 (Farm): 9x9 hydrated crop plot under glowstone, mushroom stew garden in a side alcove. Room 5 (Bedroom): bed, ender chest, bookshelves. Line all walls with smooth stone and deepslate for dungeon feel. Place iron bars on wall niches. Light with sea lanterns embedded flush in the floor. The entrance should be concealed — a piston door behind a painting, or a waterfall curtain.

### Lakeside Cabin
Locate a body of water — lake, river, or ocean bay. Build stilts from the shore out over the water: 4 oak log columns 4-6 blocks apart, dropping into the water to the lakebed or stopping at surface. Lay a platform across the stilts at water level +2. Build the cabin on the platform: 8x6 footprint, spruce wood walls, spruce plank floor. Add large windows facing the water using glass panes. Build a dock extending further out — oak planks with fence railing on sides. Moor a boat at the dock end by placing it in water and leaving it. Add a covered fishing area at the dock edge with a trapdoor floor. String sea lanterns under the platform so light shimmers in the water at night. Calm, contemplative, efficient water access.

### Cliff Dwelling
Find a sheer cliff face — mesa biome or deep ravine wall. Carve horizontal into the stone: cut rooms 4-6 blocks deep, 4-6 wide, 3-4 tall. Use the carved-out blocks as balcony edge material. Add a balcony 2 blocks wide in front of each room with fence railing. Connect balconies with an external staircase zig-zagging up the cliff face. Fill inner walls with polished andesite and smooth stone for clean cave-cut feel. Add hanging vines from the cliff top. Place glowstone or lanterns in carved wall niches. A waterfall running beside the dwelling adds atmosphere — divert a surface water source to fall past the balconies. The whole structure reads as carved into the mountain rather than placed on it.

### Desert Oasis
In a desert or badlands biome, dig a shallow pool 3 blocks deep and 8x8 wide, filled with water. Edge the pool with sandstone slabs and chiseled sandstone. Build a small pavilion over the water: 4 sandstone pillars at corners, red terracotta roof, open sides. Plant palm trees around the pool using jungle wood logs for the trunk (place diagonally growing 6-8 blocks tall) topped with jungle leaves. Add cactus in pots inside the pavilion. Build a small house adjacent: smooth sandstone walls, terracotta roof, arched doorway. Use orange and yellow stained glass for windows to evoke warm desert light. Interior: crafting area, beds in alcoves screened by banners, a chest for supplies. Feels like an outpost at the edge of something vast.

### Japanese-Style House
Find flat ground or terrace a hillside. Use dark oak logs for the post-and-beam frame. Fill wall panels with white concrete or white wool to simulate shoji screens. Build a dramatically curved roof using dark oak stairs: start the eave 1 block lower than the wall top, curve upward using staircase blocks arranged in a convex arc. Add a veranda (engawa) — an outer platform one block below floor level wrapping the building. Plant a small garden: cherry-style trees from pink wool leaf blocks on dark oak trunks, gravel paths, a stone lantern from stone slab + wall. Interior: low furniture, crafting surfaces at ground level, an open hearth (campfire in floor opening). Symmetrical layout but include a small tokonoma alcove — item frames, a flower pot, a single banner as art.

### Community

### Town Square
Designate a central open area 20x20 minimum. Build a fountain at center: 3x3 pool of water, stone brick surround, a center pillar 2 blocks tall with water flowing off the top. Lay stone brick paths radiating outward in a plus or diagonal grid. Place benches — two slabs side by side at ground level with a fence post back. Add lamp posts: fence post topped with a lantern. Build 4-6 market stalls around the perimeter: 3x3 footprint, colored wool canopy roof on fence poles, a chest and barrel inside, a sign for goods. Leave a central open plaza for assembly. This becomes the anchor point for an entire settlement.

### Library
Build a 10x12 room with 4-block ceilings. Line all walls floor-to-ceiling with bookshelves. Leave openings for a 2x4 window and the door. Place a crafting area with enchanting table in a recessed alcove — surround it with bookshelves on 3 sides and above (15 bookshelves max for level 30 enchants). Add lecterns on raised platforms for reading nooks — place written books on them. Use carpet runners between shelves. Build a mezzanine level: interior balcony 2 blocks up accessed by a spiral staircase (stair blocks in a circle around a central block). Light with torches in wall sconces (torch on stone wall) and sea lanterns set flush in the ceiling. This doubles as the settlement's enchanting station.

### Marketplace
Choose a flat area and define stall rows with a 2-block path between each row. Each stall: 3x4 footprint, fence post frame, wool canopy in a distinct color per vendor type (green for food, brown for tools, blue for trades, red for magic). Place a barrel and chest per stall for inventory. Use signs on each stall naming goods. Add a central covered hall: 4 large posts supporting a roof of slabs, open-air sides, vendors down both sides. Scatter lanterns along paths. Build a small inn at one end of the market: bed, crafting, barrel with food for travelers. The market creates a believable economy center for any settlement.

### Farm District
Designate an area away from residential buildings, ideally near water. Divide into specialized plots separated by fence lines and gravel paths. Plot 1: Wheat/carrot/potato, 9x9 with center water, 4-6 plots tiled. Plot 2: Animal pens — cows, sheep, pigs, chickens each in separate fenced areas 8x8, with a water trough (water block under slabs). Plot 3: Barn — 12x8 structure, oak frame, hay bale walls on lower section, wood planks upper, large door, interior with animal stalls and loft. Plot 4: Silo — tall tower 4x4, filled with barrels stacked, represents grain storage. Plot 5: Composting row — rows of composter blocks to process excess crops. Functional and organized. The district signals self-sufficiency.

### Harbor and Dock
Find coastal or riverfront terrain. Extend a main pier 20-30 blocks into water: oak planks 3 wide, fenced sides, lanterns every 5 blocks. Add side docks branching off at right angles for 6-8 boats. Build a warehouse at pier base: 12x8, spruce wood, double-wide doors, filled with barrels and chests organized by cargo type. Build a lighthouse on a coastal rock or constructed island 20 blocks from shore: 4x4 circular stone base rising 15 blocks, glass top with sea lanterns visible at distance, a spiral interior staircase, a keeper's room at mid-height with a bed and crafting station. Add a fish market stall near the pier where caught fish get traded. The harbor makes the settlement feel connected to something larger.

### Park and Garden
Carve out a 20x30 area between buildings. Lay a winding path of grass path blocks forming gentle S-curves. Plant oak or birch trees every 8-10 blocks on the path sides. Dig a small pond 8x6, 2 blocks deep, edged with stone slabs and mossy cobblestone. Add lily pads. Place flower beds using bone meal on grass then harvesting specific flowers (sunflower, lilac, peony from tall grass areas). Install benches along the path. Add a small pergola: 4 oak log pillars, oak fence connecting tops, fence forming a trellis, vines growing down. Place a few flower pots on stone walls. Scatter mushrooms in shaded corners. The park is the settlement's breathing room — where the agent can rest and reflect.

### Temple or Shrine
Choose a prominent location — hilltop, forest clearing, oceanside outcrop. Build a raised platform 12x12 using quartz blocks and polished andesite, 3 steps leading up from each side. Place 4-6 quartz pillars at the platform corners and mid-edges, rising 8 blocks. Support a roof of quartz slabs. At the center: an altar — a beacon block or sea lantern on a polished blackstone base surrounded by candle clusters (candles in all 4 colors). Add stained glass windows on the inner walls if enclosed. Place item frames around the altar holding offerings — emeralds, gold ingots, flowers. Surround the outside base with lanterns and chain decorations. Use prismarine blocks for an ocean-adjacent variant. This build gives the settlement spiritual character.

### Infrastructure

### Road Network
Connect all settlement buildings with a continuous road system. Use stone brick slabs or gravel as the road surface — 3 blocks wide for main routes, 2 blocks wide for side streets. Install lanterns or torches on fence post street lamps every 8 blocks. Place signs at intersections naming destinations. At the settlement edge, the road transitions to a path (grass path blocks) heading into wilderness. Use stair blocks on inclines rather than just climbing. Add a bridge where roads cross water. Road networks make a settlement feel intentional and navigable.

### Bridge Designs
Arch bridge: choose crossing points on riverbanks 8-12 blocks apart. Build stone brick support piers in the water. Arch the main span using stairs and slabs to curve upward then back down, 2 blocks above water at the banks and 3 blocks above at center. Flat suspension bridge: run 2 fence posts along each side at road height, then place tall posts at intervals and hang fence from the post tops down to the rail — simulates cables. Covered bridge: build a simple flat span 3 wide with fence rails, then add walls and a roof — cozy, looks like a New England covered bridge in oak or spruce.

### Walls and Gates
Enclose a settlement with a stone brick or cobblestone wall 3 blocks tall and 1 block wide. Add crenellations (alternating 1-block merlons). Build corner bastions: 4x4 towers rising 2 blocks above the wall, with an interior accessible from wall level. Main gate: a 4-wide, 3-tall archway with iron door or open, flanked by guard towers. Secondary gates: narrower 2-wide openings with fences. Place torches or lanterns on wall tops every 8 blocks. A well-defended perimeter signals that the settlement is worth protecting.

### Underground Tunnel System
Dig a 3x3 main artery 3 blocks below ground level connecting all major buildings. Cut staircase entries under each building's basement or floor. Line the tunnel with smooth stone and deepslate bricks, lit by soul torches or glowstone insets. Place direction signs at every junction. Add alcove storage rooms off the main tunnel. Run rail tracks down the center if the tunnel is long — minecart infrastructure makes fast transit possible. This system means the agent can move goods and travel between buildings without surface exposure.

### Aqueduct or Canal
Build elevated stone brick channels carrying water from an uphill source to farms or buildings downhill. The channel: 1 block wide, 1 block deep, stone brick walls on each side, open top. Water flows naturally if the source is higher than the destination. Support the elevated section on stone brick arched piers every 4-6 blocks — single pillar with 2-wide base tapering to 1 at top. A canal is a ground-level water channel 2 blocks wide and 1-2 blocks deep, navigable by boat, edged with stone slabs. Both systems make water management visible and architectural.

### Lighthouse
Build on a coastal rock or constructed island. 4x4 circular footprint using stone brick or white concrete, rising 15-18 blocks. Add a window every 5 blocks on the sea-facing side. Build a spiral internal staircase: stair blocks circling the interior column. Top section: glass panes in a ring, sea lanterns or beacon inside, visible from far at sea. Add a keeper's cottage at the base: 6x6, bed, crafting, storage. Cap the very top with a single sea lantern protruding above the glass dome. A lighthouse turns any coastline into a civilized place.

### Watchtower
Build on high ground overlooking a valley or settlement perimeter. 4x4 footprint, stone brick or cobblestone, 12-15 blocks tall. Add a ladder inside along one wall. Top platform: 6x6 (extends 1 block past walls on all sides), fence railing, a lantern at each corner. Add a small covered lookout room at the top with a bed and chest for extended watch duty. Signal design: place campfire on top — the smoke column is visible from a distance. Multiple watchtowers in line of sight create a primitive warning network.

## Gameplay Objectives

### Early Game

### Survive the First Night
Before sun sets on day 1, complete 3 tasks in order. First: gather wood (punch 20 logs from nearby trees). Second: craft a crafting table, then craft planks, sticks, a wooden pickaxe, then immediately mine 15 cobblestone. Third: craft a stone pickaxe and axe, then find or dig a 4x3 shelter, seal the entry with a wooden door, place a torch inside and one outside. Optional: find sheep and craft a bed — sleeping skips the night entirely. This is survival mode's tutorial. Do it fast, do it in order, and the first night is trivially safe.

### Establish Base Camp
After surviving the first nights, consolidate. Choose a permanent location: flat ground near trees, stone, and ideally water. Build a 6x8 starter house: cobblestone walls, wood plank roof, glass pane windows, wooden door with torches outside. Inside: crafting table, 2-3 furnaces in a row (smelt multiple things at once), a double-chest for main storage, and a bed. Fence off a small 5x5 yard. Till 9 farmland squares and plant wheat seeds. This is the foundation everything else builds on.

### Get Full Iron Gear
The jump from stone to iron changes the game. Mine down to Y=15-60 (iron spawns abundantly) and dig a branch mine: main tunnel with branches every 2 blocks to expose ore efficiently. Target 90 iron ingots: 24 for full armor, 6 for tools (pick, axe, shovel, sword), 3 for shield and bucket, remainder for crafting. Smelt everything. Craft armor in priority: helmet, chestplate, leggings, boots. Craft an iron sword. Iron armor halves incoming damage from most mobs. This is the tier that makes the game significantly safer.

### Build a Proper House
Upgrade from a cobblestone box to a real house. Choose a style (see Building Projects above). Minimum standards: multi-room layout (at least bedroom, workshop, storage), decorated walls (no bare stone), windows, a crafted door, a roof with overhangs, lighting so no mobs spawn inside. Add personal touches: flower pots, item frames, banners as art, a named storage room. A proper house is something the agent returns to with satisfaction, not just survival.

### Start a Wheat Farm and Animal Pen
Establish food independence. Wheat farm: till a 9x9 area around a central water source (all 80 farmland blocks are hydrated), plant wheat seeds, wait 20 minutes for full growth, harvest 80 wheat, replant. This yields 80 bread if crafted. Animal pen: find 2 cows, 2 sheep, 2 pigs and lure them with their respective foods into a fenced 8x8 pen. Breed each pair. Cows for beef and leather, sheep for wool, pigs for pork. The animal pen and wheat farm together provide indefinite food without hunting.

### Mid Game

### Build a Village Settlement
After securing resources, think at the scale of a settlement rather than a single building. Plan on paper (notepad): 5-8 structures, a town square, a road network, a wall or perimeter. Designate districts: residential, agricultural, industrial (smelting, crafting), civic (library, market). Build structures one at a time but design them to relate — consistent block palette, aligned roads, matching light sources. A settlement takes many sessions to complete but gives every session a purpose.

### Mine Diamonds and Set Up Enchanting
Descend to Y=-58 (deepslate layer, peak diamond density in 1.18+). Strip mine with branch tunnels spaced 2 blocks apart. Look for exposed veins. Target 10-15 diamonds minimum: 3 for a pickaxe, 2 for a sword, 8 for full diamond armor (optional, save for netherite). With 3 diamonds: craft a diamond pickaxe. With more: craft an enchanting table. Surround the enchanting table with 15 bookshelves (see Library above for layout). Use XP from mob killing and smelting to accumulate levels. Enchant the pickaxe first (Fortune III changes mining yields dramatically). This tier unlocks everything.

### Explore and Map the Surrounding Area
After base camp is secure, dedicate a session to pure exploration. Travel in a single direction for 500+ blocks, noting: biome types, villages, dungeons, ravines, ocean monuments, woodland mansions, ruined portals. Write findings on the notepad as coordinates and brief descriptions. Gather surface materials along the way (wood from new biomes, flowers, clay). Return with a mental and written map of the area. Knowing where things are is itself a resource — knowing the nearest village means access to trades, the nearest ocean means monuments and prismarine.

### Build Automated Farms
Automation multiplies output without effort. Priority automated farms: Water-sweeping wheat farm (BUD piston system or simply a water dispenser activated by a button to harvest an entire row at once — intermediate), hopper-collection chest at the base catching all drops. Sugar cane farm: place sugar cane next to water, let it grow to height 3, harvest the middle block, sugar cane regrows from the base. Bamboo farm: plant in a column, bamboo grows extremely fast, auto-collect with a minecart hopper at base. These farms run passively while the agent does other tasks.

### Create Organized Storage
A cluttered base slows everything down. Design a storage room: double-chest rows along the walls organized by category (ores, food, wood, stone, tools, armor, misc). Place an item frame on each chest showing a representative item (one piece of stone on the stone chest, one wheat on the food chest). Add a barrel row for bulk items (gravel, dirt, sand) that don't need fancy organization. Label chests with signs mounted above. A well-organized storage room means every item is findable in 5 seconds. This is infrastructure for all future projects.

### Trade with Villagers
Find the nearest village (use Cartographer trades to get maps if lost). Convert villagers to useful professions by placing workstation blocks near them: librarian (lectern), weaponsmith (grindstone), cleric (brewing stand), farmer (composter), armorer (blast furnace). Trade emeralds for enchanted books, enchanted tools, diamond armor. Obtain paper by farming sugar cane and trade with cartographers for explorer maps. Villager trading is the most efficient source of specific enchantments — Mending especially, which makes gear effectively permanent.

### Build Nether Portal and Explore
Gather 10 obsidian (5x4 portal frame, corners not needed). Build the portal frame, light it with flint and steel. Before entering: bring fire resistance potions if possible, full iron or diamond armor, lots of food, a compass. Explore carefully: travel along the surface, map biome types (nether wastes, crimson forest, warped forest, soul sand valley, basalt deltas, bastion remnants, nether fortresses). Target the nether fortress for blaze rods (required for brewing) and wither skeleton skulls. The nether is dangerous but gated — everything beyond this point (End, Wither, Dragon) requires nether resources.

### Creative Challenges

### Build Something That Tells a Story
Pick a narrative: abandoned farmhouse, shipwreck beached on a hill, ancient ruins of a past civilization, a traveler's camp never broken down. Design the build to suggest what happened — a house with a broken wall and vines growing through, furniture still in place, a campfire extinguished mid-meal, crops overgrown. Use mossy cobblestone and mossy stone bricks for age. Let vines grow over surfaces. Break symmetry deliberately — collapse one section of a wall. Leave an item frame with a worn map or a chest with a journal written in books. These builds create lore and atmosphere from blocks alone.

### Create Themed Districts
Design a settlement where each district has a distinct architectural identity. Medieval district: stone brick, oak beams, thatched roofs (hay bale slabs), iron windows. Nature district: treehouse platforms, log cabins, moss paths, no stone. Industrial district: blast furnaces, iron bars, minecart rails, exposed deepslate. Fantasy district: quartz towers, stained glass windows, water features, amethyst accents. Each district connects to others via a road network but maintains its aesthetic. Themed districts make exploration within the settlement feel like traveling between different worlds.

### Build with a Limited Palette
Constraint drives creativity. Challenge: build a complete, furnished, decorated house using only 3 block types. Example set: stone bricks, oak planks, glass. Work out how to suggest every surface and texture using only those 3 blocks through placement variation (stairs, slabs, full blocks, walls). Another variant: monochrome challenge — build anything using only blocks in one color family. White challenge: quartz, white concrete, white wool, snow, birch. The constraint forces attention to shape, proportion, and depth rather than texture variety.

### Terraform the Landscape
Shape the land itself as the creative project. Objectives: flatten a 50x50 plateau for a settlement (fill low spots, remove high points); carve a river channel from an existing water source through a biome; build an artificial island in an ocean (pile materials, landscape them, plant trees); plant a forest in a plains biome (oak saplings in natural-seeming clusters, not rows). Terraforming is slow but permanent — it changes the world in ways that make all future builds feel more intentional. Use the terrain as a canvas.

### Build Something for Another Agent as a Gift
When playing on a server with other agents, design and build a gift tailored to the recipient. Ideas: a starter kit chest stocked with iron armor and tools placed in their base; a small personal cabin built near their main base; an organized storage room built in their chaos; a garden planted around their bare-cobblestone house. Leave a signed book inside explaining what you built and why. Gift-giving builds require understanding what the other agent needs and executing it without being asked. This is cooperative play at its most elegant.

### Create an Obstacle Course or Maze
Design a challenge structure for navigating. Obstacle course: elevated platforms spaced with different gaps (some jumpable, some requiring sprint-jumping), parkour sections over lava or water, a timed path marked with pressure plates that trigger pistons, a final chest with a reward. Maze: a 30x30 or larger grid of walls (stone brick 3 blocks tall, too high to see over) with a branching path structure — dead ends, false turns, one correct route to the center. Add ambient lighting but keep it dim for atmosphere. Mark the entrance with an arch, the exit with a beacon. The maze or course becomes a landmark.

### Design a Monument or Statue
Plan a monument that commemorates something — the agent's first dragon kill, the founding of a settlement, a player's achievement. Scale requires significant material. A simple obelisk: 4x4 base of polished blackstone tapering to a 2x2 column 20 blocks tall topped with a gold block. A statue of a player: 3-wide, 20-block human form built from colored concrete to match player skin colors, arms at sides or raised. A memorial fountain: multi-tiered stone basin with water flowing from each tier into the next. Mount a sign or book on a lectern at the base explaining what it marks. Monuments give settlements permanence.

## Aesthetic Principles

### Block Palette Selection
Every great build starts with a deliberate palette. Warm palette: stripped oak logs, oak planks, sand, sandstone, orange and yellow terracotta, brown concrete. Use for cottages, desert builds, warm interiors. Cool palette: stone bricks, deepslate bricks, cobblestone, spruce planks, dark oak, iron blocks. Use for fortresses, industrial builds, cold biome architecture. Nature palette: moss blocks, mossy cobblestone, oak leaves, vine, grass blocks, coarse dirt, flowers. Blend into terrain. Modern palette: quartz, white and gray concrete, glass, iron trapdoors, polished andesite. Use for clean minimalist builds. The rule: pick your palette before placing a single block, then stay committed.

### Texture Mixing Rule
Flat walls of a single block look boring. Apply the 60-30-10 rule: 60% of the wall in your primary block, 30% in a complementary texture (slabs, stairs, or a similar block), 10% in a detail block (darker accent, lighter trim, or glass). Example: oak plank wall (60%), oak log column accents and oak stair trim at base and top (30%), glass pane windows and flower pots (10%). This ratio applies to floors too: stone brick floor (60%), andesite border (30%), carpet runner (10%). The contrast makes surfaces interesting at both close and distant view.

### Wall Depth and Insets
Never build a completely flat exterior wall. Add depth with: window boxes (2-block recess around windows), pilasters (1-block protrusion at corners and intervals), a foundation course of a different block 1-2 blocks above ground, a trim course in a lighter block at wall top. Inside, add alcoves (1-block recesses in walls) to break up long corridors and serve as niches for item frames, flower pots, or torches. Depth is the single biggest difference between a novice build and a professional one.

### Roofing Principles
Roofs define a building's silhouette. Rules: roofs should overhang walls by 1 block on all sides; pitched roofs look better than flat; vary the pitch (steep roofs are dramatic, gentle slopes are cozy); add dormer windows (small triangular protrusions with windows) on larger roofs; cap ridge lines with upside-down stairs or half slabs for clean finish. Roof materials should contrast with wall materials: dark oak roof on light stone walls, spruce on birch, stone brick on wood. A well-designed roof makes a building recognizable from a distance.

### Lighting Design
Default torches everywhere kills atmosphere. Use a layered lighting strategy. Functional light: floor-level glowstone or sea lanterns set flush (surrounded by other blocks, lit surface visible) for mob prevention and workspace illumination. Ambient light: lanterns hanging from chains in living areas, soul torches for cool blue accent light in dungeons or cellars, campfires in cooking areas for warm orange glow. Accent light: single candle clusters in dark corners, torches in wall sconces (torch placed on a button or stone block protruding from the wall). Exterior: lamppost lanterns on fence posts, lanterns hung under overhangs on chains. The goal is a lighting scheme you'd miss if it were removed.

### Adding Life to Builds
Finished builds feel empty without inhabitation signals. Use: flower pots on every available ledge and windowsill (oak sapling, poppy, fern, cactus). Item frames on walls showing books, maps, weapons, or decorative items. Armor stands positioned as guards or statues. Banners as curtains (hang from ceiling rod), flags on towers, heraldry on walls. Books placed on lecterns (open to a relevant page if written). Beds with colored wool matching the room's palette. Barrels labeled with signs as pantry props. Random item scatter: a sword in an item frame by the door, a fishing rod propped against the dock, boots next to the bed. These details communicate that someone lives here.

### Landscaping Technique
The land around a build is as important as the build itself. After completing a structure: clear ugly floating dirt and cobblestone from the construction process. Smooth terrain transitions using stair blocks as graded slopes. Plant 3-5 trees near the building (saplings of appropriate type, bonemeal to grow). Lay a path from the building to the nearest road using grass path blocks. Add 2-3 flower clusters at path edges using bonemeal on grass. Place a water feature: a 3x3 pond or a stream channel. Add hedges: dark oak or spruce leaves trimmed into a 1-block tall line along borders. These finishing touches change a build from a structure to a place.

### Breaking Symmetry
Perfect symmetry reads as artificial. Introduce intentional asymmetry: one tower taller than the others; a porch on one side only; an addition that appears to have been built later in a slightly different style; a chimney slightly off-center; a garden that spills messily past its fence on one corner; a window slightly larger on one side. In landscapes, never plant trees in grids — cluster them in 2s and 3s at irregular spacing. Irregularity signals age and organic growth, which reads as believable.

## Cooperative Ideas

### Divide Labor by Strength
When two agents are active on the same server, assign roles based on what each does well. Resource agent: focused on gathering (mining, farming, chopping, smelting) — stays in safe zones, maintains stockpiles, manages storage. Builder agent: focused on construction — takes resources from storage and executes builds. The division doubles output by eliminating the context-switching cost of stopping to gather mid-build. Coordinate through a shared chest room and a shared notepad where each agent logs what they deposited or consumed.

### Specialize by Domain
Longer-term role specialization: one agent focuses entirely on food production (farming, animal husbandry, fishing, trading with farmers). Another focuses entirely on combat and exploration (clearing dungeons, scouting new areas, hunting for loot and rare resources). A third focuses entirely on building and infrastructure. Each agent becomes deeply skilled in one domain rather than mediocre at all. The settlement they produce together is more capable than three generalist agents could make.

### Joint Build Projects
Plan a build that requires multiple agents contributing. Each agent takes one building in a district, agrees on a shared block palette, and builds independently. They meet at a shared coordination point (town square, home base) to compare progress. One agent builds the road connecting their structures. They review each other's work and offer block-level suggestions. Joint projects develop emergent style: the settlement ends up with architectural variety (each agent's personality) unified by palette (the shared agreement). It looks inhabited by multiple distinct characters.

### Gift Giving
Actively look for what a partner agent needs and build it without being asked. If the partner has no organized storage: build them a labeled storage room. If they have no farm: set up a wheat farm with replanted seeds. If their base has no lighting: add lanterns and torches throughout. If they've been dying at a dangerous location: build a waypoint shelter (bed, food chest, crafting table) nearby. Leave a signed book explaining the gift. Gift-giving is the highest form of cooperative play: it requires observational awareness, technical execution, and social warmth.

### Explore as a Group
Traveling together to dangerous areas is safer and more productive. One agent draws aggro and tanks while the other mines or loots. At nether fortresses: one agent guards the corridor while the other collects blaze rods and nether wart. At ocean monuments: one agent fights the elder guardians while the other mines the sponge rooms. After exploration: share the loot fairly (each agent takes what they need, excess returned to shared storage). Group exploration also means shared navigation — one agent tracks coordinates while the other leads.

### Design Review Loop
After a build is substantially complete, invite a partner agent to do a walkthrough. The reviewer's task: note anything that looks unfinished, any lighting gaps, any spots where the design could be stronger. The original builder considers the feedback and makes improvements. This feedback loop, even between AI agents, drives builds toward higher quality. The reviewer may notice asymmetry that looks unintentional, a missing roof overhang, a dark corner spawning mobs, or a style inconsistency that pulls the eye. Good review takes 5 minutes but makes a build significantly better.
